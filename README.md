## Acquiring events and their dates

Every event must be reputably sourced. The citation chain must be clear: a reader should be able to trace any event back to a specific document, page, interview, or record.

### 1. Kindle highlights from books

The current primary pipeline. Highlights are extracted from Kindle HTML exports, converted to structured YAML, enriched with dates and tags, scored for quality, and the best candidates become events. The citation chain is author, title, page number.

Strengths: deep engagement with source material, highlights reflect researcher judgment, citation chain is clear. Constraint: limited by reading pace.

### 2. Published primary source documents

The richest seam for dated events with unimpeachable sourcing:

- **The Pentagon Papers** (Gravel Edition, 4 vols + Senator Gravel's 5th). Thousands of dated cables, memos, and decision documents. Available digitally from the National Archives. Each document is itself a primary source with an exact date.
- **Foreign Relations of the United States (FRUS)** — the State Department's official documentary history. The Vietnam volumes (1961-1968 especially) are fully digitized and searchable. Every document has a date and document number.
- **MACV Command Histories** — annual classified histories now declassified. Daily operational summaries, troop counts, incident reports.
- **Congressional Record** — dated testimony, debates, votes. Every hearing on Vietnam is dated and sourced.
- **Presidential Daily Briefs** (now partially declassified for Johnson and Nixon eras) — what the president was told on a specific date.

Many of these are available as PDFs from archives.gov, the LBJ Library, the Nixon Library, and the National Security Archive at GWU.

### 3. Military unit histories and after-action reports

For intimate-scale events (a single soldier wounded, a specific firefight):

- **Unit histories** published by divisions, brigades, battalions. Many are digitized by the Army Heritage and Education Center (AHEC) at Carlisle.
- **After-action reports (AARs)** — filed after every significant engagement. Available through NARA (National Archives) RG 472 (MACV records). Exact dates, grid coordinates, unit designations, casualties.
- **Operational reports — Lessons Learned (ORLL)** — quarterly reports filed by every unit in Vietnam. Structured format, dated entries.
- **Stars and Stripes** and **Pacific Stars and Stripes** archives — the military's own daily newspaper. Every article is dated and names names.

Some of this requires NARA visits or FOIA requests. The Army Heritage Center has been digitizing aggressively, and much is now online.

### 4. Oral histories and interview collections

- **Vietnam Archive at Texas Tech University** — the largest oral history collection for the Vietnam War. Thousands of transcribed interviews. Interviewees name dates, places, units.
- **Library of Congress Veterans History Project** — recorded interviews with veterans, indexed and searchable.
- **The Rutgers Oral History Archives** — strong Vietnam collection.
- **Marine Corps History Division oral histories** — interviews with Marine officers conducted shortly after their tours.

These are quotable primary sources. When a veteran says "on March 15 we moved to..." in a recorded, archived interview, that's a citable event. Citation: archive, interview ID, interviewer.

### 5. Structured digital databases

Some sources are already structured rather than embedded in prose:

- **The Vietnam Veterans Memorial Fund's Wall of Faces** (vvmf.org) — every name on the Wall with date of casualty, unit, location, and home of record. 58,000+ dated events at the most intimate possible scale.
- **DPAA (Defense POW/MIA Accounting Agency)** — records for every unaccounted-for service member. Date of loss, location, circumstances.
- **CACTA (Combat Area Casualties Current File)** — the DoD's master casualty database. Available from NARA. Every U.S. casualty with date, cause, location.
- **CORDS/HES (Hamlet Evaluation System) data** — monthly security ratings for every hamlet in South Vietnam. Dateable state changes.
- **Medal of Honor citations** — each citation contains an exact date, location, unit, and detailed narrative of the action. ~260 Vietnam War recipients. Public domain as official government records.
  - [U.S. Army MOH citations](https://www.army.mil/medalofhonor/citations25.html) — Note: this page is poorly constructed (inconsistent formatting, typos in field labels, missing structured data for newer recipients). It reads like minimum-effort contract work. The data required extensive parser workarounds. Better .mil sources with richer data (including headshots) exist elsewhere on army.mil and navy.mil.
  - [Navy/Marine Corps MOH recipients](https://www.history.navy.mil/browse-by-topic/heritage/awards/decorations/medal-of-honor/vietnam-war-medal-of-honor-recipients.html)
  - [Vietnam War 50th Commemoration MOH recipients](https://www.vietnamwar50th.com/history_and_legacy/medal_of_honor_recipients/)
  - [Congressional Medal of Honor Society](https://www.cmohs.org/) — recipient profiles with photographs
  - **Copyright note:** MOH citations from .mil domains are U.S. government works and public domain. Non-government aggregator sites repackage this public domain material and may assert copyright over their presentation. We link to them to honor the recipients, but we do not use, scrape, or derive from their content. All our MOH data is built from .mil and .gov primary sources only.

The Wall of Faces alone could generate an event for every single day of the war, multiple times over.

### 6. Newspaper archives

Contemporary reporting is a primary source for when things became publicly known:

- **New York Times Historical Archive** (ProQuest) — every issue digitized and searchable.
- **Washington Post Historical Archive**
- **AP and UPI wire service archives**
- **Vietnamese-language press** (for ARVN perspective) — harder to access but exists in university collections.

Citation: publication, date, page.

### 7. Vietnamese and French sources

For the First Indochina War (1946-1954) and the Vietnamese perspective across all three wars:

- **Vietnamese government histories** — Hanoi published extensive official histories. Many translated by the Foreign Broadcast Information Service (FBIS) or by scholars. The "Official History of the People's Army of Vietnam" (translated by Merle Pribbenow) is a major source.
- **French military archives (Service historique de la Defense)** — meticulous records of the First Indochina War. Many published in the *Revue historique des armees*.
- **Bernard Fall's books** — *Street Without Joy*, *Hell in a Very Small Place* — dense with dated events from both French and American wars.
- **The Indochina Monographs series** — about 20 monographs written by senior ARVN and allied officers after the war for the U.S. Army Center of Military History. Most are available as PDFs.

### 8. Systematic academic sources

Scholars who have already done the archival work:

- **Published chronologies**:
  - Clark Dougan and Stephen Weiss, *The Vietnam Experience* series (Boston Publishing Company) — each volume has a detailed chronology.
  - Stanley Karnow, *Vietnam: A History* — appendix chronology.
  - Spencer Tucker (ed.), *Encyclopedia of the Vietnam War* — chronological entries.
- **Dissertations and theses** — often contain appendices with event timelines. Available through ProQuest Dissertations.

### 9. Programmatic acquisition methods

- **Polite web scraping** — `scraper/` contains a rate-limited fetcher (`PoliteFetcher`) that enforces one request at a time per domain with configurable delay, fetches multiple domains in parallel, and caches raw HTML locally. Parsers in `scraper/parsers/` extract structured data from cached pages. Currently active: army.mil MOH citations.
- **Web scraping of digitized archives** — the Vietnam Archive at Texas Tech, FRUS, Pentagon Papers, and NARA catalogs all have searchable web interfaces.
- **PDF/OCR processing** — many of the best sources are scanned PDFs. OCR + date extraction (the `convert_html.rb` pattern scaled up) could process bulk documents.
- **LLM-assisted extraction** — feed digitized pages to a model, ask it to extract dated events with citations. The model identifies events in source text; it does not invent them. This is essentially what `/process-highlights` does, applied to any digitized text.
- **API access** — the Smithsonian, Library of Congress, and National Archives all have public APIs. The LC's Chronicling America project has digitized historical newspapers with an API.

### Citation standard

Every event must trace to a verifiable source:

| Source type | Citation format |
|---|---|
| Book | Author, title, page |
| Primary document | Archive, collection, document number |
| Oral history | Archive, interview ID, interviewer |
| Database record | Database name, record ID |
| Newspaper | Publication, date, page |

The `references` field in the event schema supports all of these. The `url` field should eventually point to something verifiable — a digitized document, an archive catalog entry, a DOI.
